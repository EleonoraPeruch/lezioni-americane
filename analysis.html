Semantic analysis

The levels of interpretation of the "unsaid"

Different semantic levels are layered in Calvino’s works. These levels contain, in a hidden way, elements that bring value to literature for our author. I decided to conduct a wide-ranging analysis of these different semantic levels across a variety of sources: Italo Calvino’s narrative works, his essays, the critical literature on his literary production, and the theoretical landscape characterising the years during which Calvino wrote, in the attempt to extract some common features that could allow me to better define and characterise this concept of the hidden and its value for Calvino. I then started by applying the results of this first research on the first lecture of Calvino’s Lezioni americane.

The semantic interpretation of each memo depends on multiple levels and modes. Each level is then further defined by a series of elements of interest, as shown by the tags branching below. The choice of the types that contributed to the definition of each level was in part established during a first research, a priori, following intuition, background knowledge, suggestions, and in part extended or modified during the research on the tested lecture.

The historical background

The social and historical context influenced Calvino’s literary production considerably. As a writer and intellectual he always felt the duty to engage with the facts of reality and provide readers with a way to do so as well. Not only has he used literature to represent the facts of reality, in some cases facts that he himself experienced, but also to help individuals, readers, to reflect over reality and understand it. For the great influence that it had on Calvino and his literature, history is present more or less explicitly, and it is a meaningful presence. For this reason, one of the levels of the unsaid in Calvino and his Lezioni can be identified with the historical reality emerging from or related to the texts.

The cross-references

With cross-references I mean any reference to other authors made by Calvino in his works. In most of the cases the reference is explicit, containing the mention of the author and/or the work. Lezioni americane is particularly rich of cross-references to authors who have served as models or examples to Calvino and have influenced his writing style and construction of the discourse.

What is interesting about a cross-reference is what the author has in their mind about that other author when mentioning them or their work, for example which is the passage from their text or an idea that our author means with the reference. This is the unsaid at the level of cross-references; not the cross-reference itself, but what stands behind it.

The cognitive level

In this level I consider the process of catching and understanding the knowledge of the world by means of literature. This is the reason why I decided to include the field concerning the relation between reality and the writing process, but also the structure given by Calvino to the discourse. Indeed, following the purpose to represent the complexity of the real, Calvino’s reasoning and writing process starts by following a linear itinerary from a place to another, which eventually creates a network, a tangle. In the attempt to reproduce the reality on the written page ambiguity occurs, and when there is ambiguity something is not said correctly or not said at all. The unsaid here is the complexity of the unwritten world, as well as, from the perspective of literature, the potentiality of the writing process in representing it.

Symbolic objects are used to communicate to the reader in a tangled but simplified way the reflection over reality. They can be considered as means through which the author tries to organise and communicate to the reader the unsaid as referred to in this level of analysis.

The interpretative level

The meaning of Calvino’s text is not pre-established, but rather built by each reader with their own interpretation. In fact, the reader cooperates with the author in the construction itself of the work. We could explain it in this way, as if the work carries a message initially started by the author and continued by the reader through the reading and interpretative process. In this sense, writer and reader are co-authors of the work. Since the meaning of the work depends on both of them, part of it is likely to be found within the text, however another part of it is unsaid: it will be brought to the fore by the reader. Calvino’s texts include many reflections on the relation between the author and the reader, a continuous dialogue with creative potential.
